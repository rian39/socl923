Week 3 – Textual and Semiotics Analyses of ‘Fake News’

The group discussed two contrasting texts of ‘fake news’, one is YouTube video whilst the other is online news report.  We conducted semiotics and textual analyses on both texts which will be seen below.

First text – “Clive Anderson Shot Dead”

The first video discussed by the team was a fake news report by Channel 4, a British public-service television broadcaster, declaring the death of TV presenter and broadcaster Clive Anderson, allegedly shot dead by television host Noel Edmonds (Brass Eye, 1997). After a woman news reader in a grave voice, announces the killing of Anderson by Edmonds, a woman reporter with a fake moustache is shown telling viewers about the police siege, outside Edmond’s residence where his lookalike is shown firing a rocket launcher at  a wedding banquet and is then shown throwing Anderson’s head out of the window.

In this text, the semiotics of – the set of the news room, the authoritative tone of the news reader, the placement of graphics and the camera angles used, were all replicated to represent an actual news broadcast.

People interviewed for reactions on the news were not aware that the whole thing was staged. While the actual verbatim used by the news reader had a comical undertone, there were different ways in which the audience reacted to it: while some viewers took it literally and believed the news to be real, some viewed as a satire (Free Online Library, 2017).The textuality of this news broadcast apart from the real life ‘appearance’, especially with respect to the aspect of timing, creating a break from the usual routine, generated varied reactions of shock, contempt, interest and laughter among the viewers.

Second text – Kellyanne Conway and the “Bowling Green Massacre”

In the wake of the recently proposed travel ban on seven Muslim majority countries, by the American president Mr.Donald Trump, Kellyanne Conway- counselor to the president, supported this ban by referring to a “Bowling Green massacre”, a massacre which never actually took place. The news was telecast in the Hardball, an American talk show hosted by Chris Matthews on MSNBC channel. Conway quoted, “I bet it’s brand new information to people that President Obama had a six-month ban on the Iraqi refugee program after two Iraqis came here to this country, were radicalised and they were the masterminds behind the Bowling Green massacre. Most people don’t know that because it didn’t get covered” (Guardian, 2017a).

In this text, the semiotics of – the interview broadcasted at the fixed time in a real set, Conway delivering the answer with absolute seriousness, the interviewer not cross questioning Conway, all contributed to the news to be considered as ‘real’.

Criticizing the false news, residents of Bowling Green expressed dismay at the attempt made by the Trump campaign to reinforce stereotypes about certain groups/ communities, especially those concerning refugees practicing Islamic faith. It is essential to look at the “text” used in order to delve deeper to decipher the deeper meanings of this false piece of news. Dr. Patricia Minter of Western Kentucky University, aptly analyses the incident and observes that the choice of words made by Conway is deliberate and loaded, both politically and culturally with terms that are designed to frighten people into fearing a certain group, in this case- Iraqi refugees, or in this case, the entire refugee community (Guardian, 2017b).

Parallels could be drawn to a similar incident in India which took place last year, when the current government stirred up a media led hysteria over a alleged anti- India (doctored) speech made by the student leader of the prestigious Jawaharlal Nehru University (JNU) which led to mass protests by the public enraged over attack and arrests of the concerned students (BBC, 2017). The text- in this case, the video-audio manipulation was done in a manner to appeal to the public consciousness on nationalism and to create negative opinion about the said university and its students, which was a part of a broader political agenda targeting the university, faculty and its students.

Couldry (2000) defines text as one that involves “a complex of interrelated meanings which its readers tend to interpret as a discrete, unified whole” and defines textuality as “different ways in which something can function as a text for its readers” (p.70-71).Textual analysis covers analysis of any text which can include all verbal-written text, images, videos, paintings etc and can involve any kind of analysis within it- semiotics, compositional, visual, discourse analysis or ethnography or live methods. Semiotics can be considered as a part of textual analysis in which signs and symbols act as the ‘text’, it refers more to the visual text and meanings are thus derived from the used symbols, visuals or signs used.

From the videos that we studied as a part of the week’s group work, we conclude that the depending on the extent  to which an individual/audience engages with ‘news’ in ever day life he/she will react differently  to satire or news that appears ‘fake’ and to news which does not appear to be overtly ‘fake’. Both semiotics and text will therefore affect the consumer of fake news differently, depending upon his level of engagement with the media form.The group felt that in view of the instantaneous nature of online/mass media , it is easier to spread false news without any factual evidence in order to create the desired effect, by any vested interest group. Often such news is released when the environment is already tensed and volatile, fuelling further unrest and fear among the public.
