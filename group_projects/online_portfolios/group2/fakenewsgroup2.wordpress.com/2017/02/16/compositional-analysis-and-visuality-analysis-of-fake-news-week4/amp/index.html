Week 4 – Compositional Analysis and Visuality Analysis of ‘Fake News’

mediaresearchgroup2
2 months ago

This week’s discussion is regard to the compositional analysis and visuality analysis of ‘fake news’. Two visual entities are selected as examples of ‘fake news’. The first one is a journalistic photograph which won the first prize of spot news in the contest of 2013 World Press Photo. The second is a screenshot from a imitation show of Donald Trump and Hillary Clinton’s debate on ‘SNL’.

The award-wining photo Gaza Burial in question, an image by Swedish photographer Paul Hansen shows a group of men carrying the bodies of two children killed in an airstrike through Gaza City. According to the results of a forensic analysis published by ExtremeTech, it might be a composite of three different photos spliced together (see appendix) and then altered to illuminate the faces of the subjects.

This is a central placed image. The group of people block the street in Gaza city. The bodies of two-year-old Suhaib Hijazi and his elder brother Muhammad, almost four, are covered with plain white cloth and carried by their uncles to a mosque for their funeral. Men in the image are filled with deep sorrow: they are bowing their heads and crying bitterly for the lost of young lives, raising their  hands and shouting to protest against airstrike. There are stone buildings without window on the two sides of the narrow old street. The photo has a vertical feeling with two boys in front and in the very deep depth of field, electric wires connected to the telegraph pole are all in a mess.

The overall tone is saturated. The front men all are in dark and the colour of background is in low degree of saturation. Because the group of people are standing in a narrow street with high buildings on both sides, the natural light is from the top right corner to the left bottom of the photo. In this way, half of men are in light and the rest are in the shadow. The viewer’s position is in front of the group of people and a little higher than them in order to look further.

For people who are local to such war-ridden places, they might recognise this photo as a common image. They are so shocked, frightened, or upset for a long time that they cannot think clearly or feel any emotion about war, about the lost of family members. This makes me think of a picture of a five-year-old boy saved in ruins in Syria. He is totally dusty and with blood on head whereas his expression varies from poker-faced to blank. Perhaps he has been numb to war. On the contrary, if people live in peace, war is not something they can see in daily life, they might feel shocked and angry of it. This kind of emotional relations and shock experience may promote the circulation of such visual entities and let more people be aware of the topic about war and peace. In this way, this kind of photo can appear in the contest of spot news, in the poster and website of the anti-war organisations, and also in the social media platforms.

The second example is a screenshot from a imitation show of Donald Trump and Hillary Clinton debate on ‘SNL’. The video shows two performers who look like Trump and Clinton having a discussion. In the picture Trump (Alec Baldwin) is on the left side, in a black suit and with humoristic expression. On the other side Clinton (Kate McKinnon) is wearing a red suit. She has a perfect makeup and looks really elegant and graceful. The artificial light in the news room gives both of them a bright appearance. The dark blue background provides a distinction effect of the front figures. Viewers of this picture is just a face-to-face position.

This video is live from Hofstra University. People can get access to it on Youtube, social news website (e.g. Digg), and entertainment websites (e.g. Entertainment Weekly). As above-mentioned websites support their viewers to share content to other social platforms, people can also see the video in Twitter and Facebook. From the different portrayals of Trump and Clinton in the picture, it is clear that the political stand of the video maker is to support Clinton. As for people who are Clinton loyalists, they may get great fun of this video and are willing to share the content in their own social media. But for Trump supporters, they may feel disappointed to this or even get angry. Overall, different emotional relations of this picture is according to people’s political stand and whether or not they are concerned about American Presidential Election.

In sum, the compositional analysis is more about close viewing. It focuses on rich description of the visual entity and does not say much about meaning or connotation. The analysis of visuality involves in ‘what happens when people look and what emerges from the act’ ( Morra & Smith, 2006, p.273). A kind of performativity of practice is related to seeing and looking.
