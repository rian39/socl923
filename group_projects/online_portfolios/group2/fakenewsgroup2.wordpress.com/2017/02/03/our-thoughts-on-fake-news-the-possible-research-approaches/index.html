Week 2 – Our Thoughts on ‘Fake News’ & the Possible Research Approaches

This week we discussed the topic of ‘fake news’ further, specifically our own personal experience with ‘fake news’. We also tried to clarify our understanding of what ‘fake news’ is and whether ‘fake news’ emerged as a result of the 2016 US Election or whether this phenomenon has always been a part of society as we dealt with the varying spectrum of ‘fakeness’ in the news on a daily basis for decades. This week we also came up with three empirical materials related to fake news that demonstrate and explain what ‘fake news’ is to us, these are shown below:

“Trump Claims Waterboarding Doesn’t Come Close To The Excruciating Torment He Experiences At Every Moment”

This very provocative headline came from the news satire website, TheOnion.com. To those who are aware that The Onion is a media company and a news satire organisation, this headline is not being taken seriously as we know it is written with humour, irony, and exaggeration. However, those who are unaware of this would take this headline at face value and consider this piece of information as true and accurate. As a matter of fact, on the 29th of January 2017, Sean Spicer, the White House Press Secretary retweeted a video called “5 Things to Know about Sean Spicer” that was tweeted by The Onion with the caption “@SeanSpicer’s role in the Trump administration will be to provide the American public with robust and clearly articulated misinformation,” an unfortunate misunderstanding that resulted in his being ridiculed by countless of people online.

“How’s Google Doing In Its Fight Against Fake News?”

This second headline came from NBC News website where the news article summarised how Google is reviewing more than 500 websites and restrict ads from more than 300 sites for violating their policies for “misrepresent, misstate, or conceal information about the publisher, the publisher’s content, or the primary purpose of the web property.” Google’s policy update is a step in the right direction to combat the prevalence of ‘fake news’ leading up to and after the 2016 US Election – we can only hope that Facebook, as well as other affected sites, follow suit.

“Fake News Website”

This last source is a Wikipedia page that is dedicated to ‘fake news’ websites. The page provided a definition of ‘fake news’ where the main agenda is to “mislead, rather than entertain readers for financial, political, or other gain” – it even distinguished ‘fake news’ from news satire. The page contained a large amount of information on ‘fake news’ coverage, its definition, history, sources and impact amongst other things. Wikipedia may not be the most reliable website but it is a good starting point if you want to learn more about the ‘fake news’ phenomenon.

None of us had experience ‘fake news’ with regards to the 2016 US Election but we are all aware of the issue because it has been in the news. We all agreed that the concept of ‘fake news’ predates the 2016 US Election, we believe that there had always been a varying degree of ‘fakeness,’ i.e. inaccuracies and biases, in the news. Sometimes we have many questions as we struggle to know what is real; what is fake; who is reporting it; what gets reported; who controls the media; what about ownership of media. Because of the current media climate, we have come to expect that not all news reports are 100% accurate. We need to adjust our perception and use our judgment to decide whether the news is reliable, accurate and valid.

Easter, who is from China, experienced ‘fake news’ on Weibo (China’s hybrid version of Twitter and Facebook) where some articles are based on fabricated celebrity interviews with falsify sources and information which caused lots of confusion and misunderstanding among their users. Whilst Kausiki, who is from India, is aware of spoof and parody sites, she is also very aware of the biased and inaccurate reporting that exists within India’s media industry. She offer an example of a media company in India that is owned by an individual whose agenda is influenced by the political party that is in power. This resulted in interference and manipulation of news reporting which aimed influence and bend the public’s opinion. For me, I am certainly aware satire news, gossip sites, and tabloids, both in the UK and Thailand. I have also experienced how media companies in Thailand are influenced by certain political parties and the state which resulted in biased news reporting. Like many other countries in Asia as well as other parts of the world, censorship is something that is a fundamental part of news reporting in Thailand whilst freedom of the press and freedom of speech remained an unfamiliar concept.

To conduct a research study on ‘fake news,’ there are many different approaches that may be suitable. Because the materialist approach viewed culture as the result of what particular people have done at a particular time and place, under certain constraints and experience. It is possible to research ‘fake news’ using this approach to explore the production of ‘fake news’; their motivations behind producing these fake news – is it merely for monetary gains or is there a bigger agenda at play?  It is also possible to explore people’s experience with ‘fake news’ because even though we may view the same ‘fake news,’ we may react differently and have contrasting opinions about it.

Moreover, there are other approaches that may be suitable, one of those is affect theory. It would be very interesting to explore ‘fake news’ in relations to affect. We might be moved by the stories or it might invoke a negative affect of disgust towards the subject in the ‘fake news.’ It would be fascinating to investigate the role that ‘fake news’ play in the 2016 US Election. Because of the ‘sticky’ tendency of affect, the negative affect of a figure, such as Hilary Clinton, may build up negative emotions associated with her that could escalate to the feeling of disgust towards anyone with a liberal view or those who subscribe to the Democrat Party. Conversely, the research could also take on a Marxist view with a focus on the underlying economic motivation behind ‘fake news’ where the research on the commodification and ownership of media regarding the production of news stories and the agenda of those who produced it, would be a very interesting albeit challenging take on ‘fake news’.
