Week 1 – What is ‘Fake News’?

mediaresearchgroup2
2 months ago

Prior to the lecture on 20/01/17, we had all heard of the term “fake news” but none of us really had a good understanding of what that phrase actually meant. We understood it to means “news stories which are not real”, but all agreed that stories like that have been around for a long time, yet the term “fake news” has only recently been coined in the aftermath of the Donald Trump election. In this project, we aim to demonstrate the significance of “fake news” in the media and cultural studies landscape.

We discussed some themes that came up around the idea of “fake news”, and talked about the following three points:

Context – the idea that “fake news” only seems to be called as such when read in a certain place or when being passed off as a genuine story, for example, someone who reads the National Enquirer or a celebrity gossip magazine probably does so already accepting that these stories are “fake” or deliberately taken out of context. We take them with a pinch of salt.

Filter – the idea that on social media, we are unable to filter where news comes from and so we might read a “fake news” story with the same sense of legitimacy that we would read an article from a reputable news source like the BBC. This is possibly because we often see news stories through second or third hand accounts, through sharing. Your first encounter with an article might be through it being shard by a friend of a friend, and so you read the article without a firm grasp of the source, and whether that source is legitimate or not.

Agenda – the idea that ALL news is “fake news” to an extent, in that it’s extremely difficult to read about stories in a truly unbiased way, as every media outlet has it’s own agenda to push, be it the conservative, right wing leanings of the Daily Mail, or the liberal, left wing views of the Guardian: Every media source makes you view the story through the lens that it holds up for you.
