The two research questions for this research project are :

  1. What is the discursive constitution of ‘fake news’?
  2. What is the relation between the construction of credibility and the consumption of ‘fake news’?

The table below shows the research plan for this 3-month long research project on ‘fake news’.

As shown in the table above, the project has been divided into five different stages:

  1. Literature Review

In the first three weeks of the project, each group member will conduct further background research on the topic of ‘fake news’. Past and current Media and Cultural Studies research on the topic of ‘fake news’ will also be explored. The scope of the research and research questions may be revised at this stage.

  1. Collect Empirical Materials

Additional texts will be collected at this stage to complement the sources already collected. The texts collected will be from various sources including:

  • Tabloids e.g. The Sun and The Daily Mirror
  • Gossip Magazines and Websites e.g. National Enquirers and Heat Magazine
  • Mainstream media sites e.g. CNN and BBC News
  • Satire program e.g. Saturday Night Lives and The Daily Show with Trevor Noah
  • Newspaper e.g. The Daily Telegraph, The Guardian and The Daily Mail
  1. Analysis of the Empirical Materials

We will analyse the material individually using the most appropriate method, which may include semiotics, textual, compositional, visual or discourse analysis, in addition to ethnography and live methods, to answer the research questions posed in this research project.

  1. Writing the Research Report

The writing up of the report will happen at this stage. The result and findings of the analyses will be discussed in the report.  The implications and conclusions will also be drawn here.

  1. Critical Reflection

We will reflect upon the analyses that we conducted during the research project and make any revisions and changes where applicable.

Research Rationale

Research Question 1: What is the discursive constitution of ‘fake news’?

In the beginning of the group project, the group discussed how there have always been varying degrees of fake-ness in the media, whether it is through tabloids, celebrity gossip sites, news satire sites and programs, different newspapers with different political & social perspectives – all of which predated the 2016 US Presidential Election. Therefore, in this research study, we will be using a multitude of methodology that are frequently used in Media and Cultural Studies research to analyse the empirical materials that we will be collecting in the course of the project and explore the spectrum of fake-ness within the ‘fake news’. We hope to find different types of ‘fake news’ with varying fake-ness, such as satire, rumour, gossip, unsubstantiated statements, and ‘fake news’ that is related to the 2016 US Presidential Election, to name a few.

Research Question 2: What is the relation between the construction of credibility and the consumption of ‘fake news’?

At the same time, we would like to explore how the different components of ‘fake news’ can contribute to the its credibility, which will subsequently influence how the audience consumed ‘fake news’. Examples from ‘fake news’ websites would include:

  • Name of the Website: some ‘fake news’ website have very similar names to a legitimate news outlet which can fool an untrained eye into thinking that the news is in fact part of the mainstream media landscape.

  • Appearance of the Website: several ‘fake news’ website look very similar to a mainstream news outlet website and contain no clue to indicate that the website may be ‘fake news’ or satire. The Onion is one of the example of this as its website project legitimacy and for those who are unaware that this is a news satire site, there will be a difference in judgement used when consuming the content of this site

By employing the various methods of analysis in this project, we will be able to identify the different elements of what constructs ‘fake news’  that lends itself to the legitimacy and credibility of ‘fake news’.

Ultimately, we are attempting the explore the spectrum of fake-ness in ‘fake news’ in the contemporary media landscape as well as the how the construction of credibility in ‘fake news’ sources can alter the public consumption of ‘fake news’ in a post-truth age.

The group studied and analyzed the transition of the website of the fast food restaurant chain McDonalds over a period of 17 years ie. from 2000 to 2017, on the Internet Archive Wayback Machine. This method allowed us to empirically collect images of McDonalds website to form a visual essay to observe the changes in dynamics over a long period of time and see the larger picture of the organisation.

History of McDonalds:

Richard and Maurice McDonald opened “McDonalds Bar-B-Q” in California, USA in 1940 and its catapulted popularity led to its countrywide expansion to 102 outlets, by 1959. In 1966 McDonalds aired its first ever TV commercial and introduced the new face of the brand Ronald McDonald which was an instant hit and led to the creation of a new demographic of customers targeting children. In 1979 the Happy meal was invented targeting this new target group. Mcdonalds soon became synonymous with cheap and tasty meals, often criticized for its unhealthy high-sodium and high- sugar content. The documentary “Super-size me” released in 20004, highlighted the negative effects of a McDonalds-only diet. In response to pressure from childhood obesity groups, the brand started to promote healthier meals. In 2014, for the first time in a decade, sales went through a decline. Soon after the brand opened an office in Silicon valley to focus on digital innovation and a transparency campaign. Whether the brand can successfully reinvent itself as a promoter of healthy food and healthy eating remains to be observed. (FastCompany, 2014)

Year: 2000

The homepage of the website in 2000, reflects a basic layout with almost no picture of food, devoid of any mention of nutrition facts. The look and feel was in tune with the static design characterized in the Web 1.0 phase, in which user interaction was minimal and information was heavy. The red brand color is retained in the website as well.

Year 2005:

With the advent of the Web 2.0 phase, 2005 saw a major transformation in the website in terms of technicality as well as in message communication. Through the use of the Flash software, the website was made more user-friendly and interactive than the previous versions. The three paneled division in the centre of the homepage displays varied images covering diverse groups like children, mixed race groups, young and the old, implying the brand’s different target groups. Specific subsections focusing on Food, Nutrition and Fitness, Corporate Social Responsibility, make prominent appearance which the group felt was a reflection of the brand’s effort to transform the identity; from that of a cheap-unhealthy food brand towards one that promotes a healthy and nutritious food and to one that promotes inclusiveness by embracing diversity among people. The image of the brand as a ‘fun’ brand to be enjoyed with friends and family is also reflected. ‘Transparency’ in communication is also demonstrated along with a focus on the ‘humane’ nature of the brand. The colour panel of the background changes from Red to Black.

Year 2010:

2010 reflects a major upheaval in terms of the look and feel of the website through animation created through programming/plug- ins as well as in message communication. Photographs of real food make an appearance along with attractive promotion of different food and beverage categories. People behind the food are introduced to dispel myths about the food offered. For eg. On being asked about the long-term goal for the McDonald’s menu, Chef Dan whose interview is published in the website replies that it is “to change people’s perceptions about our food not being real.” Values such as sustainability, recyclability-renewability, animal welfare etc. are highlighted. The continuous effort, as evident from the website, to focus on the ‘realness’ of the food and the emphasis on the authenticity of the food procured along with ‘values promotion’ can be read as a defense against the negative image of the brand perceived by the public. The background color shifts from Black to Grey.

Year 2013:

Retaining the overall look of the website, 2013 saw a slight modification in terms of more direct communication related to promoting healthy meals among children, ‘realness’ of the food and on the brand focus on CSR initiatives. The Left panel promotes Happy meals for children by focusing on healthy additions like apples and books to fit into the market as a brand that is concerned about children’s well being. The middle panel at the bottom links to the profile of the different food suppliers, retaining the brands effort to promote the authenticity of the food offered by introducing the ‘people behind the food’.  Efforts of the brand to position  itself as a ‘responsible’ brand by promoting charity and well being, is also re-emphasized by the addition of the right panel on CSR initiatives. The background colour shifts to Dark blue.

Year 2017:

Keeping pace with the user friendly interface adapted by most companies, by 2017, the website too implemented all features to make its website mobile/user friendly and easily accessible through promotion of mobile Application and by introducing videos, multi grid homepage layout, attractive visuals by retaining its focus on health-nutrition facts and by introducing value-for-money deals. Communication is also highlighted through focus on social media platforms like Twitter, Facebook and Instagram. The background panel color is changed to black.

The group observed that the brand underwent major changes over the years, in terms of the maturity in content, visuals, design-layout and user-interface. The brand has made a consistent effort to change its identity from an unhealthy fast food chain to one that promotes healthy food and is concerned about its customers and the society. It is worth contemplating as to what extent the brand has succeeded in its attempt and whether this constant effort is reflected in its actual sales.

“I want to understand the world from your point of view. I want to know what you know in the way you know it. I want to understand the meaning of your experience, to walk in your shoes, to feel things as you feel them, to explain things as you explain them. Will you become my teacher and help me understand?” – James Spradley, (1979)

What is Ethnography?

In essence, ethnography is the practise of studying people and cultures directly – it is a way of experiencing the world. From a sociological perspective, ethnography allows us to approach the study of culture in different ways, both reflectively and internally.

The internal approach means that we immerse ourselves within a culture, essentially blending into the background in order to try and understand it from the inside. We become part of the culture, and study it’s norms, traditions, rules, structures, etc… Ideally, this kind of study should have an air of the banal about it; the immersion into the culture should be natural to the point of uninteresting in order to try and make the observation as authentic and accurate as possible.

The reflective approach involves us writing from the perspective of an outsider – it’s our position as the “other” in a culture unfamiliar to us that offers us insight into how that culture works, perhaps as a direct contrast to what we are more familiar with. Our potential alienation – discomfort, even – offers us a point of reference that was can use to explore our understanding further.

Why Ethnography?

When adopting more traditional, formal, “hands-off” means of cultural study, there can be a certain barrier that is thrown up when subjects are fully aware that they are being studied. What people say and project and what they actually do can be two totally different things, so in order for a study to be properly authentic, that barrier needs to circumnavigated. Ethnography is a means of doing just that: By immersing ourselves into the culture we are studying, there is a sense of a “guard being dropped”, and the true nature of the study is revealed.

From an ethical standpoint, “being there” represents the best opportunity to present a proper, true and sensitive account. It also gives a voice to “the other” or cultures that might otherwise be overlooked and unheard by mainstream culture.

Ethnographic Methodology

Critical to the ethnographic method of study is the field site – the environment in which the study is to be carried out.

In the early days of anthropological study, the field site often referred to some far-off, exotic country where new cultures, often tribes, had been discovered and were being studies. In the modern day, the field site can be anything from a remote tribe, to an online messageboard, to the micro-cultural practices on display in high street shops… Essentially anything can be considered a field site. However, whilst anything could potentially be considered a field site for a study, there are often problems that are thrown up in terms of accessibility – can we actually access the site? Do the people there want to be studied? Is immersion even feasible?

In Class Discussion

Relative to our discussion on Ethnography, we talked about possible ways of employing a field site study into the subject of fake news, the overarching theme of this module. We considered various areas that could be useful, and arrived at the conclusion that some sort of study into the pro-Trump, Alt-Right community might provide an interesting environment in which to undertake research into the understanding of “fake news”. Specifically, we wondered if the idea of “fake new”s as we have come to understand it – concisely summed up in an advert for the New York Times, “The Truth is Hard” – tallies with the understanding of fake news from an American, right wing perspective. We suspected that perhaps it does, but that fake news as a tool of propaganda and media wrangling is proving so influential that the confusion around it is actually desirable, as it provides a powerful means to promote a conservative agenda.

With reference to the in class exercise, we came up with the following proposed areas of research for an ethnographic study.

Places:

NRA Meetings in Texas – Political views leaning one way or another are a crucial part in the nationwide identity of American states, and a group such as the NRA is fiercely pro-Republican and largely indicative of the American Right Wing.

Alt-Right Online Forums – Online political forums represent an opportunity to study people’s viewpoints, often unfiltered through the enabling factor of online anonymity.

Anti Trump Protest Marches – This is where you likely find the most vocal and vociferously opposed people to the Trump office, to look at alternate views and opinions.

People:

NRA Members – Often made up of ordinary, working, Republican, Southern Americans. They could present an opportunity to get the views of the average Republican voter.

Moderators – Online forum moderators would provide an interesting source of study, as their attachment to certain types of forum indicate an interest or approval of a certain set of beliefs, but their capacity within that as moderators means that they have to recognise inappropriate behaviour and comments in others who share similar beliefs.

Protestors – It would be useful to study the background, education, lifestyle and economic circumstances of protestors in contrast to the backgrounds of the NRA Members to see if there are predispositions to certain beliefs based upon those factors.

Artefacts, Practices, Events:

NRA Lobby Groups – Studying the political weight of the NRA in relation to their influence on proposed laws, and how they try and shape the political landscape based on the beliefs of the organisation.

Online Video Artefacts – We would look at the types of videos watched and shared by the types of people who would populate Alt-Right forums.

Women’s Rights Marches – We would posit that there is a shared sense of political beliefs between people that are firmly anti-Trump, and people who are active in social rights movements.

Problems of Access:

Whilst ethically dubious, it’s very easy to adopt any sort of persona you want on an online forum in order to immerse yourself and even participate in its culture and to view its practices. This would likely prove to be the easiest way of carrying out an ethnographic study out of the three proposed, but could result in inaccuracies in the conclusions drawn, because of the anonymity factor, the lack of interpersonal connections, and other aspects of interpreting meaning such as body language, etc…

Protest marches are done explicitly with the intention of making a particular point or being heard and are held worldwide, so getting protestors to air their opinions in an environment that promotes that shouldn’t prove too hard. Within that, however, there is the issue of physical access. A lot of protest marches are chaperoned by the police, and limited to very specific areas. There is also the threat of a protest ending in unrest and, in extreme cases, violence.

This week, we were introduced to discourse analysis, a key method in Media and Cultural Studies. Discourse analysis is a system of things and meanings that can be used to make sense of the textual and media environments which is never isolated. Discourse analysis always looks at the broader picture and formation where all media are mixed and there is no such thing as ‘visual media’. To Foucault, discourse analysis involves groups of statements that structure how things are thoughts and how actions are based on those thoughts where there is less emphasis on meanings and more emphasis on how power is productive and can be derived from those thoughts and actions. However, discourse analysis can include the meanings of language, images, practice, and institutions as well as the focus on the historical formation of knowledge and how knowledge changes over time. Although, it might a challenging method to tackle as one may find it difficult to focus on a specific topic as it is an all-encompassing system.

Preparing for Discourse Analysis

Before we start the discourse analysis for this project on ‘fake news’, it is imperative that we must identify the authoritative account of ‘fake news’ and the variety of sources that are related to it. In the case of ‘fake news’, the institutional apparatuses, technologies, and practices of audiencing that visually construct ‘fake news’ must be determined. For this particular analysis, we are focusing on ‘fake news’ relating to Donald Trump pre- and post-2017 US Election.

Institutional Apparatuses in the case of ‘fake news’ involving Trump will include:

  • Creators of ‘Fake News’ which include the creators of the recent ‘fake news’ phenomenon from Macedonia, Russia, Romania and the US as well as news satire websites (e.g. the Onion) and satirical television programs (e.g. The Daily Show with Trevor Noah and Saturday Night Live).

  • Experts and Scholars who often imparts their knowledge and expertise on Trump and/or ‘fake news’ on various platforms.
  • Fact Checkers who are now employed by social media sites to tackle the ‘fake news’ epidemic, in addition to websites dedicated to debunking ‘fake news’ that are on the web.
  • News and Media Outlets of traditional mass media such as newspaper, television, and magazine in addition to online and social media platforms.

  • Political Parties, both Republican and Democrat politicians as well as supporters of both parties.
  • The US Government who play their part in fuelling and/or tackling ‘fake news’.
  • Trump, this includes his immediate family, his inner circles/spokesperson/advisors namely Kellyanne Conway, Sean Spicer, Steven Bannon, and Donald Trump himself as a questionable authoritative figure and the President of the United States of America who fuelled the ‘fake news’ epidemic.

The Power Relations and Audience Engagement in ‘Fake News’

Of course, these only are some of the institutional actors in ‘fake news.’ There are, however, other non-institutional forces at play, one of which is the power relations between knowledge, truth, and power which add more questions to the process. One issue raised is the production of knowledge in a post-truth political landscape where Trump, the President of the United States of America – a leader of a country, whose sanity and statements are being questioned on a daily basis. Another key player in the prevalence of ‘fake news’ is the audience where their engagement to ‘fake news’ can be varied. For example, the audience will react differently to ‘fake news’ depending on their knowledge of US politics, their political views and their awareness of news website vs. news satire sites vs. fake news sites. Those who are adequately aware of US politics will exercise a sensible amount of judgement when faced with ‘fake news’ – the same can be said to those who are aware of news satire sites or ‘fake news’ sites, they will not take those ‘news’ seriously. The audience’s political leanings will affect their judgement in digesting ‘fake news’ as they may be biased and chose to believe a story that portrays the opposing party negatively. Moreover, the visual of ‘fake news’ and satire news sites are formatted like the sites of mainstream media which may cause confusion to the audience as they may not be able to adjust their perception and judgement in order to deal with the information provided by those sites. Furthermore, once the audience viewed and read these ‘fake news,’ there are several actions they could take. They can do nothing, talk about it with their friends and family or share it online through social media sites where the latter two would further fuel the widespread ‘fake news’ epidemic through this word of mouth and sharing process.

Ultimately, this blog entry starts to identify the institutional apparatuses of ‘fake news’ and highlight the role of audience engagement and participation in the ‘fake news’ phenomenon which is the starting point of using discourse analysis as a method to make sense of ‘fake news’ in today’s current textual and media environments.

First text – “Clive Anderson Shot Dead”

The first video discussed by the team was a fake news report by Channel 4, a British public-service television broadcaster, declaring the death of TV presenter and broadcaster Clive Anderson, allegedly shot dead by television host Noel Edmonds (Brass Eye, 1997). After a woman news reader in a grave voice, announces the killing of Anderson by Edmonds, a woman reporter with a fake moustache is shown telling viewers about the police siege, outside Edmond’s residence where his lookalike is shown firing a rocket launcher at  a wedding banquet and is then shown throwing Anderson’s head out of the window.

In the wake of the recently proposed travel ban on seven Muslim majority countries, by the American president Mr.Donald Trump, Kellyanne Conway- counselor to the president, supported this ban by referring to a “Bowling Green massacre”, a massacre which never actually took place. In this text, the semiotics of – the interview broadcasted at the fixed time in a real set, Conway delivering the answer with absolute seriousness, the interviewer not cross questioning Conway, all contributed to the news to be considered as ‘real’.

In this project, we aim to demonstrate the significance of “fake news” in the media and cultural studies landscape.

We discussed some themes that came up around the idea of “fake news”, and talked about the following three points:

Context – the idea that “fake news” only seems to be called as such when read in a certain place or when being passed off as a genuine story, for example, someone who reads the National Enquirer or a celebrity gossip magazine probably does so already accepting that these stories are “fake” or deliberately taken out of context. Filter – the idea that on social media, we are unable to filter where news comes from and so we might read a “fake news” story with the same sense of legitimacy that we would read an article from a reputable news source like the BBC.
