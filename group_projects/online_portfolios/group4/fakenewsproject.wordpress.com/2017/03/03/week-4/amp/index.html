In order to explore the method of visual analysis we shall begin by applying it to the images below.

Fake News: Compositional Analysis and Visual Analysis
Compositional analysis:
Image 1

http://cnn.com.de/news/obama-signs-executive-order-banning-pledge-allegiance-schools-nationwide/
In the image, President Obama is signing the Executive Order 13738, which revokes the federal government’s official recognition to the Pledge of Allegiance. Ten politicians are watching his behaviour, smiling. Facebook spread this fake news in 2016. Diversity is connoted through the presence of different genders and races in the image, which may convince a wider audience of the news’s authenticity. Furthermore, the colour of this image is predominantly dark, almost black, which is a basic colour of formal suits. Only the female politician is denoted wearing a light blue coloured suit, highlighting her presence. The background is indigo blue, which is a typical symbolic colour of a government press conference. The light of this image is neutral and instils a sense of documentary photography. The structure of the image resembles an inverted triangle, drawing the audiences gaze inward to Obama, whose beaming grin invokes confidence from the audience, as we are invited to share his positive emotions. The politicians standing around and watching President Obama are the top sides of the inverted triangle whilst their eye lines construct the other sides of the triangle. The whole image is filled with figures and only one fourth is background. This image is a fairly close up shot and the audience is positioned front-left to these politicians and provided a horizontal, eyelevel shot, connotations of which create a sense of openness and shared insight, as the audience gaze mirrors that of the image.
Image 2

https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/national-security/russian-hackers-penetrated-us-electricity-grid-through-a-utility-in-vermont/2016/12/30/8fc90cc4-ceec-11e6-b8a2-8c2a61b0436f_story.html?utm_term=.35bd26b9e754
Another example of fake news is the story outlined above where a “Russian operation hacked a Vermont utility, showing risk to U.S. electrical grid security, officials say” by the Washington Post. The image accompanying this headline denotes Obama and Putin staring into each others eyes while leaning inwards, connotations of which are a relationship which is cold and hostile. Also denoted in the background are their countries’ national flags, whose prominence is reinforced by the shared colour scheme of red, white and blue throughout the image, connoting the significance of national identity and difference. The dark, formal Presidents’ suits are juxtaposed with their lack of formal ties and undone top shirt buttons, suggesting  a slightly informal setting despite the implied public backdrop, connotations of which suggest that this extends beyond their leadership roles and that the pair do not share any sense of kinship outside of their work related roles. Furthermore, these connotations support the fake news headline presented within The Washington Post column itself.  The two Presidents are well lit, ensuring that they are the focus within the image, whilst Obama’s placement on the left and Putin on the right cleverly depicts their political standpoints visually, as Putin represents right-wing politics on the right, and Obama left-wing politics on the left. Readers can feel the furious spark between these two leaders, as their clasped hands rest firmly upon the arms of their chairs behind their controlled gaze, which appears to be forcibly level so as not to allow either party a more powerful downward gaze over the other. The audience is situated almost between this conflictive image as the leaders are leaning towards each other.

Visual analysis
As demonstrated above, visual analysis concentrates on the context in which an image is seen. As Marita Sturken and Lisa Cartwright (2001) argued, visual objects carry little inherent meaning; ‘one of the central tenets of this book is that meaning does not reside within images, but are produced at the moment that they are consumed by and circulated among viewers’. (Sturken and Cartwright 2001:7) Therefore, in order to analyse these two visual entities, we should attempt to co-opt the meaning it carries itself, its viewers’ interpretations and the context where it is seen.
We have already explored the ostensible meaning that is carried above through compositional analysis. It is imperative that we analyse the practices of visual objects, as argued by Rose who states that ‘practices are always embedded in specific places'(Gillian 2012: 549). The first image was spread widely on Facebook in 2016 so its seen places are fluid due to the conventions of e-media platforms, yet these places are specific to individuals and will thus vary between different individuals. As the second image was circulated in a newspaper, it is arguably not as convenient as Facebook. Viewers may read the text in public spaces such as the tube or from the privacy of their own home. Usually when audiences engage with a newspaper, they need to stay in a place where there is artificial light which requires electricity. Therefore, the latter form of fake news may have more limited forms and therefore spaces for engagement. However, ‘the space performed by practices, including visual practices, is not necessarily a simple question of location. It might also entail all sorts of other geographies, of various geometries and modalities.’ (Gillian 2012: 549). Thus in the future study, we should not bypass the potential exploration of further geographical sites which could lead us to more profound meanings of the visual objects.

References:
Gillian Rose (2012), The Question of Method: Practice, Reflexivity and Critique in Visual Culture Studies, In: Heywood, Ian and Sandywell, Barry (eds.), The Handbook of Visual Culture, London: Berg, 542-558
Sturken, Maria and Lisa Cartwright (2001), Practices of Looking: An Introduction to Visual Culture, Oxford: Oxford University Press.
