This week we explored the implications of Couldrys account of culture, power and experience. The answer to Couldrys question for media and cultural studies, “What do we hope to study”? for us is fake news, but how does one approach this subject? And what problems may this subject give rise to for the discipline of media and cultural studies?(2000: 21).

For Couldry the study of culture addresses its relation to power, arguing that in cultural studies, only the areas of research that have the methodological tools able to systematically explicate the relations between culture and power come under his definition of the discipline (2000). He also emphasizes the democratic nature of media and cultural studies, in so far as anyone can contribute to culture. An aspect of his work that is certainly relevant here, as anyone is able to create fake news, circulate it online and receive capital from people clicking on it.

The first instance of fake news we discussed occurred in china where news outlets claimed salt in china could become contaminated as a result of a nuclear leek in Japan, which resulted in lots of people rushing to the shops and buying copious amounts of salt leading, which in turn lead to salt being sold out in most places. Perhaps this story was so effective because of the history of food contamination making headline news in the past.
Further still, we felt that this news story could have been a very powerful marketing strategy produced by the salt industry, whom purposely intended to manipulate people into panic buying salt, in the wake of reductions in consumption due to health concerns. Here Couldrys concern with power and culture/media is interesting and useful for understanding this instance of fake news. Although this news story was fake, due to the history of moral panics surrounding bad food, the grounds for this fake news story to shape reality where already in place. In this way fake news is bound up with power, the power to effect the real.

Here Baudrillards work on simulacra and simulation is interesting for exploring the relationship between fake news and reality (1981). According to Baudrillard simulacra are signs that do not represent the real, instead they operate independently from reality, in this way he reverses how the relationship between signified and the signifier is traditionally thought of (1981). Indeed he argues these signs precede the real, shaping the real in its image (1981). The fake news in China that lead to the panic purchasing of salt is a prime example of how something which does not represent reality comes to effect reality. (for this article see “fearing radiation, Chinese rush to buy salt?” in the references list below)

The Second story we discussed involved an individual posing as a news reporter, getting angry and ranting about the affairs of the day. The news was true, but very biased towards a left wing ideology. The question it raised was can something be considered fake news if it is framing the truth, ergo communicating the truth but in a false way, a fake way. Here Peter Sloterdjk’s notion of enlightened false consciousness is interesting. This term refers to an ideological operation that presents the truth in a false way (1987). This example, it could be argued calls into question Couldrys contention media and cultural studies does not have to concern itself with fundamental philosophical questions (2000). The example of the fake news report telling the “truth” suggests the study of fake news requires the use of fundamentally philosophical questions not only about truth, but also as mentioned earlier with reference to Baudrillard, the nature of reality (1981)
