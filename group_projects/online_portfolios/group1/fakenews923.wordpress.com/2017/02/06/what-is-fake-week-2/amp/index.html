Week 2 Too important to ignore: Fake News

Team 1
2 months ago

In week two we have been looking at different examples of Fake News across different websites and SNSs. We focused on analysing examples of news concerning globally popular public figures involved in politics.

  • Hillary Clinton Sick with Pneumonia  

http://www.livetradingnews.com/hillary-clinton-pneumonia-14947.html#.WJ2qs7aLRsM

This is an interesting example of one of those suspicious, never proven, news. Clinton’s curious diagnosis is presented realistically, with institutional and individual voices. The direct quotations make the statements authoritative:

“Secretary Clinton has been experiencing a cough related to allergies. On Friday, during follow up evaluation of her prolonged cough, she was diagnosed with pneumonia,” her doctor, Lisa Bardack, said. 

If we critically examine the reliability of the above quotations, we will find out that doctors, just like other assistants, advisors and workers for public figures, are not allowed to reveal confidential facts of any kind (such is the pneumonia diagnosis).

There is a photograph, included in the post, which is a photoshop edited image. It hints Clinton’s health problems through the desperate, exhausted and weak look of her face.  

  • Donald Trump’s Longtime Doctor Says President Takes Hair-Growth Drug

https://www.nytimes.com/2017/02/01/us/politics/trump-prostate-drug-hair-harold-bornstein.html?_r=0

The second example that we found was this teasing article about Donald Trump – Individual and institutional voices. It aims to undermine the authority of the public figure by revealing some personal “truths” about the personal life of Trump. As in the above example, the doctor’s statement is something we should not instantly believe in. The function of Trump’s hair growth in the text is not to talk about his daily rituals but to motivate a conversation and doubts about his health in general:

At 70, Mr. Trump is the oldest person to become president.

A second letter in September 2016 was more sober, although it omitted a number of details that would be part of a customary summary of a patient’s health. The letter did say that Mr. Trump is 6-foot-3, weighs 236 pounds, has a normal blood pressure of 116/70 and takes a drug called rosuvastatin (marketed as Crestor) to lower cholesterol and other lipids.

  • Donald Trump Dead From A Fatal HEART ATTACK!

http://fedsalert.com/donald-trump-dead-from-a-fatal-heart-attack/

The third example that we discussed has very straightforward commercial character. On the one hand ,it makes a reference to Donald Trump’s vulnerability, age and health. Asks for sympathy and creates media spaces by introducing: #RIPTRUMP #TRUMPRIP #TRUMP4EVER hashtags. On the other, it has the typical features of a fake information spread with commercial purposes. The post underlines the appeal for shares:

PLEASE SHARE THIS ARTICLE TO INFORM EVERYONE OF THE SAD LOSS OF DONALD TRUMP.

This is an example of online content created with the purpose of generating profit through popularisation of the original website. It is supported with a striking photograph that will ensure our click in spite of the suspicious title and content. An examination of the photograph and how it was achieved is an essential part of the investigation of the entire article. 

  • More examples: 
  1. Materialist: culture is the result of what particular people have done at a particular time and place, under certain constraints (Couldry, 2000).                                       Couldry’s (2000) concept of materialist, is extremely useful for constructing an effective research approach, particularly focused on investigating motivation, aims and demographics. This concept is valuable because many of the articles about foreign public figures have been published and produced in Eastern European countries. It is a “popular secret” that many Computer Science and IT Technology students in these countries receive credits for successfully creating an authoritative information that can receive a global spread and popularity. It is considered a sign of exemplary skills and proficiency.
  2. The way we perceive online information greatly depends on our background and previous media encounters. I originate from a country that greatly contributes to the global Fake News”database. Most of the news produced in my country are fake with the purpose of engaging audience’s attention with certain people, brands, political movements and campaigns. We unconsciously become more familiar with some public figures: this constructs either positive or negative attitude towards them in the future. The manipulation of mind is a process that takes long time and needs a lot of carefully gathered, organised and presented information. If we try to focus on the entire chain of Fake events, rather than a single publication, we will be more able to successfully distinguish the Fake out there. I believe that people’s background and situation are crucial about the way we treat it. Our experience of fake news is interrelated to our knowledge of media across the world and ability to be doubtful about it.
  3. Theoretical Approaches: useful theoretical frameworks to analyse our selection of materials would be the Margaret Wetherell’s theory of affect and emotion and the ‘Shock’ theory  which explains the practices that result from a particular experience in the contemporary media environment (“shock as both destructive of coherent experience and as an explosive potential for new experiences of a transformed world”).
  4. Problems: while doing the analysis of some Fake News we found in different websites, we encountered a problem that is worth in-depth research: the disappearance of Fake News. Who controls Fake News and who deletes the content? Is there is a person or institution in charge of the online presence of fake news? Are there and what are the consequences for those who create Fake News? Or Fake News are just sophisticatedly organised and presented components of a more complicated global campaign for manipulation of opinions and achieving sympathy, support and popularity?
