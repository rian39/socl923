Project: The Research Project

The Research Project

Alex:
As the most familiar with the online community, Overheard at Lancaster, I will be further exploring the Facebook group. In doing this, I will be analysing the group description and content shared within the group. It is interesting to note the group is a closed group - meaning to see the content, you must be a member. This 'closed' status lends an element of privacy as user's interactions are hidden from strangers on the internet. However, with over 17,000 members in the online community, there is a large element of trust required from participants who choose to post, comment or interact within the group.

I am also the most familiar with our selected case study - the post we will be analysing in our project, Jeremy Corbyn at Lancaster University event. As a student at the University at the time the event was created (May, 2016), I was present and recall vividly the response both online and in person the event elicited. In addition, a large response was heard on the social media platform Yik Yak. Possessing elements similar to Facebook, it allows users to post anonymously, but you can only see posts within a certain radius. As such, the site creates an online community - albeit an anonymous one - as it is dependent on location.

My role in this research project is heavily to do with analysis of the wider components - the Facebook group and the event. I will conduct visual and textual analyses of these two facets of the project while my group mates will approach from the opposite direction and look at the response to the event.
 

Cheryl:
I will introduce our group and the research project, introduce the textual analysis of the response from Cat Smith, the local Labour MP to the event. This is of critical importance as while Cat Smith is local to Lancaster, she is outside of the online community and so is considered part of the wider response. She had a lot to say in reply to this event and shared her thoughts with national publications but mainly on the social media site Twitter. 

I will also be discussing the timeline of the research project. We discussed as a group how we would like to proceed with the project and what we want to do when. We designed a table to illustrate our timescale.

Yuning:
Firstly, Alex came up with the idea of doing the research on fake news on Overheard at Lancaster and analyzing how online communities interact with fake news. I really go along with this idea mainly because it can be a good place to look further in this topic and to do ethnography analysis and also partly because I am interested in researching on online community and analyzing how the concept of participatory culture is embodied in the topic of fake news.

After that, we designed the research and made a timetable together. I mainly focused on how the fake news of the event of Jeremy Corbyn Box Event was spread and the responses of it on the page of Overheard at Lancaster by using methods of ethnography analysis, textual analysis and visual analysis to look further to some specific posts. We searched on the internet to find some news on that event to have a basic understanding of that event. Given that the event page on Facebook has been deleted by the initiator, after gathering the news, I mainly focused on the mocking events and the news posted on Overheard at Lancaster.

What I am going to do is to combine the conclusion of my part together with Alex’s and Yanhui’s parts of analyzing material from wider media and Tom’s part of offline ethnography analysis and interviews of initiator and commenters. Then we will come up with our own opinion of the topic and research questions.
Tom:
For my contribution to this project, I have focused primarily on the application of Ethnographic methods to our chosen topic. Given that our area of study is a non-physical space, being a group page on Facebook, it became immediately obvious that a typical Ethnographic toolset would be of little use. In my reading, I found an example of an ethnographic study conducted entirely through online means which could act as a suitable template for our project. The paper this study resulted in, entitled Online Ethnography: A tool for dialogue between state and citizens (ACM Interactions, 2014), provides an excellent example of an observational study conducted in an entirely online space. Its authors encouraged a sample of citizens from Geneva to write blogs detailing their day-to-day lives with regard to issues affected by the Local Government.

Though the subject of the study is not directly relevant to our project, the methodological template it provides in terms of how to engage with a sample group and encouraging interactivity, both with the topics at hand and one-another, is a useful one for thinking about how we would do similar in our project. This study would be further to a “post-mortem” of sorts we would conduct examining a specific event advertised on the page, namely the Jeremy Corbyn at Lancaster University event which proved to be a deliberate falsehood conducted as part of a Fine Art student’s project in “object-oriented ontology” (Haynes, 2016). In studying this event, we would make use of textual and discourse analyses to discuss the impact this fake event had in both the digital and real world, using examples from a range of media including newspapers and television and social networks including Facebook, Twitter and Yik Yak.

Ultimately, my work in researching this project has been to find examples of Ethnography and draft a methodology to apply to our topic. I have done so through exploring a specific use of such tools in conducting a study of an online community.

Project: Research Project Timeline

Research Project Timeline

Month 1
In the first week of the first month, making a decision on how to answer the research questions would be the first thing to think about and discuss. At the same time, preparing for the interview by contacting a sample of users from the page is the part that we will mainly focus on. Further, the observational study of the page should be started in the first week. 

Then, using database like OneSearch or Google Scholar to find previous academic work on similar topics to learn would be the first step on week 2. After that, we will contact individuals from the specific event we are studying. 

On week 3, we mainly focus on gathering material from the page. Furthermore, material from wider media sources will be gathered in week 4. And reviewing and revising the topic and research questions would be the last thing we deal with in the first month.


Month 2
Moving into the second month of this research project will see us start to gather data. While the first month was spent researching, we will have identified which interviews to conduct and which posts to examine. Month 2 will see us fulfill these plans and utilise our research methods of ethnographic interview, textual analysis and visual analysis.

The first two weeks will prioritise conducting interviews and questioning participants of the online community to share information with us. With over 17,000 members, it is beneficial we have only selected one post to analyse in depth as this will reduce the number of people we will need to contact. 

The final two weeks of month 2 will involve a visual and textual analysis of the different materials we have selected. This includes the page itself, posts responding to the event, and the wider, national response to the event. Unfortunately, the original event and post advertising the event have now been deleted, so an accurate analysis can not be conducted though it is referenced throughout the selected materials.
Month 3
As the last month during the research project, we have to focus on our essay about the fake news after finishing the preparation in the first two months. At the beginning of this month, we have to form our essay plan by reviewing all the work we have done before and deciding which materials we have found in the first two weeks can be included or referred to specifically since we can’t put everything we have found in the essay. 

After setting the plan, we will need the first two weeks of this month to discuss our findings with our group mates and write the first draft of the essay together. We are going to revisit all the materials, interviews etc. we have done before and ask for a recapitulation from the sample group to make sure there are no more things we have missed in the third week before we finally redraft the essay and finish our whole project by adding any additional notes or findings in the last week in the research project.

Project: Research of Wider Affect in the Real World

Research of Wider Affect in the Real World


As a local incident happened on Lancaster campus, the Jeremy Corbyn Box Event which had been advertised on the Facebook using Jeremy Corbyn, the name of the leader of Labour Party by Lucie Carter, a fine art student at Lancaster University but finally turned out to be a hoax since the invitee Lucie claimed was actually a cardboard box with Jeremy’s name written on it had a much wider affect around the country than the event sponsor herself could image as our group found out during the research. It’s quite interesting to discover that not only did the local Labour MP Cat Smith comment directly on the event but also several national newspapers and TV news programmes reported it afterwards. Thus, we decided to study the impact of this event as a great example to indicate how the fake news shared online and then influenced the real world in people’s lives.

The Jeremy Corbyn Box Event was announced to be taken place from 13 to 16 on 18th May 2016 by the student on the Facebook. In the same morning, Cat Smith, the local Labour MP sent more than one tweets to ask the student to shut the event down using extremely serious words (http://luciecarter.format.com/jeremycorbyncardboardbox#3, http://luciecarter.format.com/jeremycorbyncardboardbox#4. [Accessed 19th March 2017]).

One day later, the Dairy Mirror reported the whole event under the title “Labour members left baffled after going to ‘Jeremy Corbyn talk’ hosted by a cardboard box”(http://www.mirror.co.uk/news/uk-news/labour-members-left-baffled-after-8008287. [Accessed 19th March 2017]). The Dairy Mirror had successfully gotten contact with Cat Smith, the local Labour MP who had firstly responded the event and asked her attitudes towards the event and the student who hold it. After that, the Huffington Post and the Guardian also reported the story from different aspects (http://www.huffingtonpost.co.uk/entry/jeremy-corbyn-cardboard-box-event-lancaster-university_uk_573ec6e8e4b00006e9ae71cc, https://www.theguardian.com/artanddesign/shortcuts/2016/may/23/jeremy-corbyn-cardboard-box-hoax-talk. [Accessed 19th March 2017]). In the report from the Huffington Post, the reporter focused on the progress of the event, especially on the various responses from Cat Smith on different occasions, the local Labour MP. The report from the Guardian was on the other side an interview with the event sponsor, Lucie Carter, asking her questions about the event and her opinion after finding out the impact spreading out of the university. 

My part of work in the group is using textual analysis to analyse these comments and reports to the event of wider coverage as they had been changing depended on the occasions and trying to answer the research question in a different way since my group mates are paying more attention on the materials online, but my focus is on responses from the real world.

Week 8: Research Questions and Materials

Research Questions and Materials

As Crang writes,

"thinking and analysis are not abstract processes or theoretical models or rules that occur purely in our heads, but involve the manipulation and orchestration of a range of materials that occur in specific places. It suggests that we need to start with the actual stuff of our interpretations, in terms of how we get to grips with (literally and figuratively) all the material we so diligently made in the field."
 
For our research project, we gathered all of the materials we plan on including and organised them in the below table to bring together different methods around our research questions.



We have suggested using textual analysis, visual analysis and ethnographic interview as appropriate methods for our research project. Some materials utilise more than one research method as contrasting or alternative ways of seeing the same topic exist.

The table is constructed in the chronological order in which we would attempt to conduct the analysis.

 Research Questions
Following this, we also finalised our research questions!

 1. How do online communities interact with fake news specific to locally relevant events and what impact do these have in the real world?

2. What are the motives behind fake news shared within an online community and how do the reasons for sharing fake news at a national or global level differ?

When our group first began discussing the topic for our research project, we immediately thought of Donald Trump in association to the topic of 'fake news'. However, upon further discussion and research, we thought it would be a more interesting study to explore fake news within a smaller field, for example fake news shared on an online community. This led to our first question - how does fake news affect the audience dependent on the source? Are people more likely to trust and believe news from a corporation such as the BBC or is a friend's Tweet just as reliable a news source?

For the second question, we then looked in the other direction of sharing fake news. The person responsible for sharing the fake news could be doing so for many reasons - they believe the news to be true, they are sharing fake news as a joke, they have no real news to share, the list is endless... We intend to investigate the sharing practices of fake news on its different scales.

Week 7: Internet Archive Wayback Machine

An Internet Archive Wayback Machine search on the official website of KFC in UK


As an American fast food restaurant chain that specializes in fried chicken, Kentucky Fried Chicken (KFC) has millions of customers all around the world. In UK, there are more than 870 KFC restaurants according to data from the official website of it. Thus, the official website of KFC in UK, https://www.kfc.co.uk/, became very popular during the last two decades. I suppose it will be a good example to research by using live methods.

The Internet Archive Wayback Machine has saved 460 times changing webpages of the official website of KFC in UK between 6th December 2000 and 8th March 2017 (https://web.archive.org/web/*/http://www.kfc.co.uk/. [Accessed 12th March 2017]). Some changes this website made were slight, for example, menu updated or advertisement replaced. However, other changes were significant enough to witness the progress this company wanted to make in these years.

The first webpage of the official website of KFC in UK the Internet Archive Wayback Machine saved at the end of 2000 (https://web.archive.org/web/20001206214300/http://www.kfc.co.uk/. [Accessed 12th March 2017]) was very simple. In the centre of the home page, there was the picture of the Colonel Sanders, the founder of this company. Below the picture, there was the classical round brand with the advertising slogan next to it, “nobody does chicken like KFC”. Above the picture, there were six white buttons on the red background which linked to other pages. The first link, “About KFC”, was the introduction of the company. Because this was the official website in UK, the introduction also contained the “UK History” of KFC. The second two links were “Food” and “What’s New”, which consisted of all the menu at that time. In the page of “Food”, some sentences in white colour were displayed to emphasize the most important distinguishing feature this company kept claiming, the “fresh” and “healthy” chichen they prepared. After them, the “Family” and “Kids” links were directed against the major customer groups of the KFC restaurants. In these pages, warm colour and sweet family picture were used to create a harmonious atmosphere to attact more customers. The last link, “Store Search”, applied a search system for visitors to find restaurants by the name of the towns or the postcodes. Though the website in 2000 was very uncomplicated, one could easily get the main ideas which the KFC company advocated by underlining various keywords. I think this website was definitely a successful one at that time.

The official website of KFC in UK didn’t change the original pages a lot until the end of 2003, but at the beginning of 2004, the website started to run a totally different home page (https://web.archive.org/web/20040228051828/http://www.kfc.co.uk/. [Accessed 12th March 2017]). The new home page was based on eye-catching red background with the new logo with the famous slogan of KFC, “Tasty, Juicy, Crunchy”, in large dark letters in the centre of the page. On the left of the logo, there was the menu link with several pictures which showed the most popular foods in the restaurants. On the right of the logo, there colourful words to publicize the uniqueness of KFC to attract visitors’ attention. The most significant change one could notice in this page was the appearance of the “KFC Careline Telephone number” and the “KFC Head Office Telephone Number”. With these numbers, customers could contact the company to reflect issues or make suggestions about the restaurants. It’s obvious that the company valued more about the opinions of the customers.

The official website of KFC in UK made a notable progress again by the end of 2008 (https://web.archive.org/web/20081023102126/http://www.kfc.co.uk/. [Accessed 12th March 2017]). At the first time, the website began to use advertising videos to fascinate visitors. It was a remarkable improvement since videos were more attractive than static pictures. Besides, the links below the videos became more standard like other official websites of companies with “Contact Us”, “Privacy Policy”, “Terms & Conditions”, “Links”, “FAQ’s”, “Sitemap”, “Accessibility” and “Store locator”. It’s clear that the KFC company needed to catch up with new Internet policies to design the official website.

In 2011, the official website of KFC in UK made a significant change again (https://web.archive.org/web/20110520235431/http://www.kfc.co.uk/. [Accessed 12th March 2017]). The home page of the website replaced the colour of the background and reduce the links to be more specific. The advertisements online started to use more keywords like “fat” and “calories” to mention their healthy ideas as well. However, in my opinion, the most fabulous advance at this time was the appearance of the links directed to Facebook and YouTube. The popularity of Facebook and YouTube was already irresistible in 2011. By displaying the links to these websites, the KFC company could propagandize their restaurants and foods, as well as interact with their customers through a wider range of channels. It was absolutely the most essential characteristic in the age of Internet. Also in the same year, the home page of the website initiated to publish public-interest advertisements, such as “Our secret weapon in the war on hunger. You.” at the end of this year (https://web.archive.org/web/20111112161725/http://www.kfc.co.uk/. [Accessed 12th March 2017]). The purpose of this decision was not only the encouragement for the customers to make the contribution to the world, but also the publicity for the culture of the company to show the positive image of it.

The recent striking change of the official website of KFC in UK was taken place in 2015 (https://web.archive.org/web/20150704105839/https://www.kfc.co.uk/.  [Accessed 12th March 2017]), while in the centre of the home page of the website, there appeared an outstanding advertisement with all capital letters, “THE COLONEL’S CLUB IS HERE!”. The colonel’s club was the new mobile application software invented by the company which could be used to collect stamps and earn free food rewards. The propaganda of the app tended to encourage customers to download the app to get benefits. Since the stamps could only be gathered after the consumption, and the free food rewards needed to be exchanged by several stamps, the app was actually a efficient tool to incite more consumption by their users. Because there are more smartphone users in the last few years, the app could make more money for the company.


By going through the home pages of the official website of KFC in UK over the last nearly two decades, I found out so many significant changes. No matter these changes are based on the technical development or the aesthetic variation, the purposes are always for the interests of the company because it is the official website run by the company itself. Hence, one can easily find out what features were most beneficial for the company in different years, and understand the impact of the Internet on the real world in the 21st century.
